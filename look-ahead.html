Four potential futures for racial equity in the United States

Though many organizations committed to racial equity in 2020, the path to get there will likely be a winding one. Without meaningful action taken now to address racial inequity, specifically for Black people, there is no guarantee that the near future will be substantially different than the recent past. And even with meaningful action, the path toward equity could take many unexpected turns or unintended switchbacks.

In late 2020, we developed scenarios designed to better understand what that path might look like. These scenarios propose different responses to the question: “How will the journey towards racial equity in the United States evolve over the next 10-15 years?”

What are scenarios and why do we use them?

Scenarios are stories about what the future may be like, created through a structured process and designed to challenge conventional wisdom, stretch thinking, and drive better decisions today. They are not predictions about what will happen, they are hypotheses about what could happen. Effective scenarios uncover new opportunities and hidden risks, helping leaders navigate uncertainty and explore options in order to make better decisions.

Many readers may be familiar with scenarios created to help leaders identify good options for a variety of plausible futures. These scenarios are different. The goal is not to understand how to thrive in any future, but to explore what’s required to create a much better future. And, practically speaking, they help us challenge our own assumptions and legacy practices in order to focus on the actions most aligned with progress toward a more equitable future.

What might shape the future?

Scenarios illuminate possibilities for the future by focusing on large, macro-level issues that are important but fundamentally uncertain. Understanding how those uncertainties could play out over time allows us to imagine how the trends we see today could generate different realities tomorrow.

Deloitte research identified a variety of uncertainties likely to shape how the future of racial equity in the U.S. unfolds. (See table 1(?)). Input from “Changemakers”—leaders in the public, private, and social sectors committed to driving racial equity—helped us identify several important uncertainties, two of which stood out as being particularly consequential.

The first uncertainty is the extent and characteristics of social cooperation across the United States. Namely, will people primarily look out for their own self-interest, and—believing that opportunity is ultimately a zero-sum game—create a fragmented society of conflicting interests? Or will they find broader and more extensive bases for cooperation, believing in more mutual benefit?

Rather than think of the two as an opposed pair of pure absolutes, it may be better to think of this uncertainty as a continuum.

The second uncertainty is the degree of economic growth over the next decade. The level and distribution of economic prosperity across regions, communities, and socioeconomic backgrounds remains unknown, and will have significant implications on whether and how American society might progress towards a more equitable future.

The simplest way to measure this is by asking whether macro-economic growth over the next decade will be relatively high or low, which in turn suggests whether or not economic opportunity in the aggregate will grow.

Understanding the implications of either one of these uncertainties on its own could illuminate useful stories about the conditions for increasing or decreasing racial equity in the future. But combining these uncertainties allows for richer, more varied, and, in some ways, more challenging narratives about the journey toward equity over the next decade—especially when a handful of other important uncertainties are woven into the scenarios, too, including:

  1. Public expectation of organizational/corporate behavior

  2. Primary measure of business performance (value for shareholders vs. value for all stakeholders)

  3. Ambition and efficacy of public policies addressing inequity

  4. Deployment of and access to digital technologies and skills

Society

• How education curricula will evolve to incorporate history of systemic racism
• Drivers of societal change (bottom-up or top-down)
• Levels of civil unrest

Technology

• Impact and distribution of work; workplace displacement from AI, robotics, and other technologies
• Role of social media in public discourse

Economy

• Distribution of economic growth across industries / sectors
• Relative economic positioning of the U.S.
• Level and durability of inequities in wealth and income
• Equitability of access to capital
• Impact of COVID-19 on prosperity of urban centers
• Representation of PEERs* across all levels of organizations

* PEER: Acronym for Persons Excluded due to Ethnicity or Race, a term used to unite people who have been historically oppressed without insinuating that those individuals are less than or that their sole identity is one of exclusion or oppression (includes people who identify as Black, Latinx, and people indigenous to the U.S. and its territories). This term shifts the focus to the acts of exclusion and oppression, while enabling individuals to disentangle their identity from it, unlike the terms “underrepresented minority (URM)” or “oppressed people.”

Environment

• Speed and intensity of climate change
• Impact of climate change on low-income and marginalized communities
• Equitability of access to clean air, water, and other environmental resources

Politics

• Collaboration across different levels of government
• Impact of immigration policy on demographics
• Ability to redirect funding for public education to high-need students
• Change in responsibilities and power of the police
• Definition and provision of social services

Scenario 1: The great unraveling

Imagine a future in which…

…some of the most challenging and divisive tensions of 2020 accelerate over next decade, spreading further and entrenching more deeply into American life. Economic decline and hardened partisan divides create sustained political turmoil and periodic outbreaks of deadly violence in communities across the country.

Widespread doubts about the electoral system mean a growing number of Americans question the legitimacy of their political opponents. High levels of populism and partisanship lead to political swings as groups fight for dominance every election cycle, but no faction stays in charge for long. The country feels ever more like a house divided against itself. Tension and distrust pushes government spending toward expanded law enforcement and increased domestic surveillance, while the social safety net is seen as an unaffordable luxury at best and an undeserved handout at worst.

Black activists and other PEERs protest deepening inequities in their communities but are fragmented in their calls to action.

Some companies have advanced opportunities for Black individuals and other PEERs, but economic inequality has worsened steadily, in part due to the lasting effects of the COVID-19 pandemic that hit Black households, communities, and businesses especially hard. Many people withdraw to focus on security for their family and those closest to them. Money is scarce; trust is even scarcer.

Many big urban centers contract as people look for safety in smaller, more homogenous communities they recognize as “their own,” further cementing division.

The urgency and energy for greater racial equity seen at the beginning of the decade is a distant memory, and the frustration many feel about the lack of progress threatens to boil over at any time.

The path that leads to this future…

• Conspiracy theories fuel divides, making it all but impossible for groups to find common ground, and putting compromise permanently out of reach.

• The movement for racial justice fragments around different strategies and different priorities.

• Social media exacerbates tensions as legacy platforms struggle to rein in the spread of misinformation, disinformation, and extremist messages. Meanwhile, new platforms emerge for communities that believe the legacy platforms are censoring their views.

• Armed groups resort to extreme measures and violence, motivated by frustration with normal politics and a sense that the stakes for their survival justify extraordinary action.

• Emigration—particularly of high-net-worth individuals—grows, as those with the ability to find safe havens for their family and their fortunes go offshore. At the same time, immigration of talented students and skilled workers drops to its lowest level in a century.

From commitment to collective action

Organizations may hope for, and voice their commitments to, a more equitable future. But in order to set the path towards that future in motion, businesses must consider the competitive dynamics governing the private sector today and step up to take powerful, courageous, collective action. Only then can they to accelerate the kind of positive, systemic change no one individual company can create alone.
