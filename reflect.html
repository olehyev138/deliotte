Why racial equity now?

Calls for action against systemic racism and racial injustice are not new. But recent events—from a pandemic and ensuing economic crisis that have disproportionately hurt Black Americans, to numerous public killings of unarmed Black people—have sparked a new era of heightened awareness, understanding, and expectation for action, including by the business community.
Sixty-one percent of Americans now believe the United States needs new civil rights laws to combat discrimination against Black Americans—a nearly threefold increase from 2011. An even greater number of Americans—77 percent—say companies must respond to racial injustice if they expect to “earn or keep [public] trust.”
Many leaders have been responding to these demands for change. A recent Fortune/Deloitte survey found that 84 percent of CEOs report that Diversity, Equity, and Inclusion (DEI) are strategic priorities for them as CEO, while over 80 percent believe their company’s DEI efforts significantly accelerated in 2020.
Despite agreeing that change is needed, many leaders have come to realize how difficult it will be to create meaningful, measurable, and sustainable change. They are left with the question: How can we make real progress toward racial equity?

Racial equity is an outcome

To start, organizations must move from focusing on good intentions to focusing on good results that can be measured against a demanding standard: equity. In the same way that business leaders focus on financial and operational outcomes rather than just inputs, they must also focus on the outcomes of their efforts to cultivate systemic change.
Racial equity for Black individuals will exist when they have the same power, access, influence, and independence as White individuals.
Equity necessitates an intentional focus on the needs of the most historically marginalized. It requires deliberate and often courageous actions that acknowledge and eliminate the societal inequities ingrained in people, processes, and systems. And it takes commitment to examining and redressing the bias and racism built into everyday decisions, which may appear fair on the surface, and which may have even been designed with good intentions, but ultimately have disparate effects on racial and ethnic minorities and other marginalized identity groups.
To achieve racial equity, organizations must be willing to transform their beliefs and values, and then change their decisions and behaviors. That work starts internally, but extends to their relationships externally in the marketplace and through to society.

“We need leaders transcending their own short-term interests for the greater long-term good.”

Steven Olikara, Founder and CEO, Millennial Action Project
